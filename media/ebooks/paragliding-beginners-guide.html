What Is Paragliding? The Sport That Changed How Humans See the World

100,000+
Active pilots globally
1978

564 km
World distance record (Honorin Hamard, 2021)

There is a moment, somewhere between the first nervous step off a grassy hillside and the sudden lifting silence of free flight, when everything changes. The ground stops mattering in the way it always has. Gravity becomes negotiable. You are no longer a creature of the earth — you are, however briefly, something else entirely. That moment is what paragliding offers, and it is why hundreds of thousands of people around the planet spend their weekends chasing ridgelines and reading clouds.

Paragliding is a non-motorised foot-launch aviation sport. The pilot sits in a harness suspended beneath a fabric wing — called a canopy or glider — that is kept aloft purely by aerodynamic lift. Unlike hang gliding, the wing has no rigid frame. It is an inflated, ram-air structure: air enters through open cells at the leading edge, pressurising the wing and giving it its characteristic shape. The result is a remarkably packable aircraft — the entire kit fits in a backpack you can carry up a mountain.

A Brief History

The sport's origins trace to 1978, when French mountaineer Gérard Bosson descended from a mountain in the Alps using a modified rectangular parachute. Within a decade, pioneers like Jean-Claude Bétemps had refined the concept, and the first dedicated paragliding schools were opening across Europe. By the early 1990s, the sport had spread to North America, South America, and Asia. Today, the Fédération Aéronautique Internationale (FAI) governs competition paragliding, with the Paragliding World Cup drawing elite pilots to venues across the globe.

Technology has evolved dramatically. Early wings were slow, unpredictable, and difficult to control. Modern beginner gliders are astonishingly forgiving, designed with passive safety systems that resist collapses and self-recover from turbulence. Harnesses have become ergonomic cocoons with airbag protectors. Variometers — instruments that measure vertical speed — have become GPS-enabled flight computers that talk you through thermals in real time.

Key Insight

Paragliding is the most accessible form of aviation. A student with zero flight experience can complete a certified beginner course in 7–10 days and fly solo at the end of it. No pilot's licence is required in most countries — only a certified training course completion card.

How a Paraglider Actually Flies

The canopy generates lift through exactly the same aerodynamic principle as a conventional aircraft wing: Bernoulli's principle combined with Newtonian reaction. Air flows faster over the curved upper surface of the wing than the flat lower surface, creating a pressure differential — lower above, higher below — that pushes the wing upward. The pilot controls direction using two brake toggles connected to the trailing edge. Pulling the right toggle deforms the right side of the wing, slowing it and pivoting the glider to the right. Pulling both toggles simultaneously induces a stall, slowing the glider for landing.

In still air, a modern beginner wing descends at roughly 1.1 to 1.3 metres per second — about as fast as a slow walking pace, vertically. To stay aloft, pilots must find air that is rising faster than they are sinking. This is the art of soaring: reading the landscape and atmosphere to locate thermals (columns of warm rising air), ridge lift (air deflected upward by hillsides), and wave systems (standing waves downwind of mountains). A skilled pilot can stay aloft for hours and cover enormous distances using energy that costs nothing and leaves no environmental footprint.

The accessibility of paragliding is one of its defining characteristics. You need no runway, no tow plane, no engine. You need a hill, a working glider, and the knowledge to use it safely. That simplicity — combined with the profound experience of free flight — is what has made paragliding one of the fastest-growing adventure sports on the planet.

The path from curious beginner to solo paraglider pilot is shorter than almost any other aviation discipline — but it requires structured, professional instruction every step of the way. Do not attempt to teach yourself from YouTube videos. The consequences of a mistake at altitude are severe and irreversible. Every legitimate training pathway begins on the ground, proceeds through low-altitude training slopes, and only graduates students to higher sites after demonstrable competence at each stage.

Day 1–2: Ground Handling and Theory

Your course begins at ground level. You will spend the first morning learning to lay out the wing, inflate it, and control it on flat ground. This discipline — called ground handling or "kiting" — is the single most important skill a pilot can develop. A pilot who can perfectly control a wing on the ground in any wind condition is a pilot who will be safe in the air. You will practice forward inflations (running into the wind with the glider rising behind you), reverse inflations (facing the wing and using the brake toggles like reins), and basic steering inputs.

The afternoons of days one and two typically include classroom sessions covering aerodynamics, meteorology basics, airspace rules, and emergency procedures. Pay close attention to the meteorology content — weather judgment is the single greatest safety skill a paraglider pilot can possess.

Pro Tip

Spend extra time on ground handling even after your course ends. World-class pilots still practise kiting in their local park. An hour of ground handling each week is worth more to your skill development than two hours in the air on a calm day.

Day 3–5: Bunny Slopes and First Flights

With ground handling under control, you will move to a gentle training slope — typically 20 to 40 metres of elevation. Instructors use radios to guide you through your first flights, which may last only 20–30 seconds each. The focus is on: a clean inflation, a controlled run, lifting off without overcorrecting, flying a straight line, and executing a flared landing. You will make dozens of these short flights. Each one builds the muscle memory and spatial awareness that longer flights require.

Day 6–8: Higher Site Flights

As your instructors gain confidence in your ground handling and low-level control, you will progress to a larger hill. Flights here might last 3–10 minutes, giving you time to practice turns, figure-eights, and approach patterns. You will learn to identify a safe landing zone from altitude, plan a consistent circuit, and manage your height to arrive in the landing area on target.

Day 9–10: First Solo Assessments

The final days assess your readiness for solo flight. Instructors observe your pre-flight checks, weather assessment, launch technique, in-flight control, and landing precision. Most reputable courses use a standardised assessment rubric. Passing earns you a P1 (beginner) certification, which is your licence to fly supervised sites and continue your education under mentor pilots.

After your initial certification, the journey accelerates. Many pilots progress to P2 (club pilot) within their first season by accumulating flight hours, completing additional training, and attending workshops in SIV (Simulated Incident in Volley) manoeuvres, where you deliberately induce and recover from wing collapses under controlled conditions above water.

Understanding Weather, Wind & Thermals

25 km/h
Max recommended wind speed for beginners
2–4 m/s

If paragliding has a single most important subject, it is meteorology. Not in the academic sense — you do not need a degree in atmospheric physics — but in the practical, observational sense. You need to understand what the sky is telling you before you launch, and you need to keep reading it throughout every flight. This chapter introduces the core concepts that every beginner must understand before leaving the ground.

Wind: The Engine and the Enemy

Wind is both what makes paragliding possible and what makes it dangerous. A moderate, steady headwind of 10–15 km/h is ideal for most sites. It gives the wing clean, consistent pressure during inflation and provides lift on ridges. But wind is rarely perfectly steady. You need to understand:

  • Wind gradient: Wind speed increases with altitude. Near the ground, friction slows the air. At 30 metres, the wind might be twice as strong as at ground level. This matters for your launch technique.
  • Turbulence: Obstacles — trees, buildings, ridges — create turbulent wakes downwind. Never fly in the lee of an obstacle. The turbulence is invisible and can collapse your wing.
  • Venturi effect: Wind accelerates through gaps between hills or valleys. Speeds can double without warning in these zones.
  • Gusts: A gust is a rapid increase in wind speed. Gusts of 30–40% above the mean wind speed are common and can violently surge a canopy. Forecast gustiness before every flight.
Critical Warning

If the wind speed exceeds your glider's rated speed range, or if gusts exceed 30% of the mean, do not fly. No exception. No "just one quick flight." Overconfidence in marginal conditions is the most common cause of paragliding fatalities.

Thermals: Your Free Elevator

Thermals are columns of warm air that rise when the sun heats the ground unevenly. Dark surfaces — tarmac roads, ploughed fields, rock faces — absorb more heat than vegetation and release that heat into the air above them, creating a rising bubble. When a thermal reaches a certain size and speed, it detaches from the ground and rises, sometimes to the base of cumulus clouds thousands of metres above.

As a beginner, thermals are primarily something to avoid — or at least to treat with caution. In strong conditions, thermals can be turbulent, asymmetric, and powerful enough to rapidly gain altitude you don't want. As your experience grows, you will learn to enter thermals gently, circle within them to climb (called "thermalling"), and use them to extend your flights significantly.

Clues to thermal location include: cumulus clouds (thermals frequently top out into clouds), wheeling birds (raptors and storks are expert thermal finders), ground features that absorb heat, and the light choppiness you feel in the glider as you pass through the edge of a rising column.

Reading Forecast Apps

The modern paragliding pilot has extraordinary meteorological tools available. Apps like XCMet, Windy, Meteoblue, and RASP provide detailed forecasts of thermal strength, wind shear, cloud base height, and boundary layer depth. Learn to read these tools before your first solo season. Cross-reference multiple sources. Look for consistency between forecasts — if three models disagree significantly, the day is uncertain and caution is warranted.

Gear Guide: What You Actually Need to Start (and What to Skip)

Paragliding gear is expensive, and the market is full of products ranging from essential to unnecessary. Understanding what you genuinely need — and what can wait — will save you money and help you avoid the trap of buying the wrong equipment for your skill level. The most important rule: never compromise on the wing and the reserve parachute. Everything else is secondary.

The Wing (Glider / Canopy)

Your wing is your aircraft. Paragliding wings are certified under the European EN standard (EN A through EN D, where A is safest and D is for experts) or the German LTF standard. As a beginner, you want nothing beyond EN B, and for your first 50 hours, EN A is strongly recommended. EN A wings are designed to resist collapses, self-recover without pilot input, and are forgiving of imprecise control inputs.

Recommended beginner wings in 2026 include the Advance Alpha 7, Gin Explorer, Skywalk Chili 5, Nova Mentor 7 Light (for lighter pilots), and the Ozone Mojo 7. All are excellent. Buy the size recommended by your instructor based on your all-up weight (body + gear).

Buying Tip

Do not buy a used wing without a porosity test. Fabric ages and becomes porous, reducing performance and safety. A reputable dealer will test the wing for you. Avoid any wing over 5 years old as a first purchase.

The Harness

The harness is your seat, your back protection, and your connection point to the wing. Modern harnesses fall into three categories: pod/cocoon harnesses (aerodynamic, protective, standard for cross-country flying), reversible harnesses (double as a backpack, great for hiking-and-flying), and lightweight harnesses (minimal protection, for experienced pilots only). As a beginner, choose a mid-range pod harness with a certified airbag back protector. The airbag deploys on impact and is dramatically more effective than foam back protectors.

The Reserve Parachute

A reserve parachute is a round or square parachute stored in a dedicated pocket in the harness, deployed in extreme emergency (complete wing failure, unrecoverable spin, mid-air collision). It is not optional. In most countries, flying without a reserve is illegal at certified sites. Choose a certified round reserve for your first wing — they are simple, reliable, and effective. Steerable reserves offer more control but are better suited to more experienced pilots.

Safety First: The Risks Nobody Talks About (and How to Manage Them)

70%

SIV

Paragliding is safer than many people assume and more dangerous than some practitioners acknowledge. The sport sits in an interesting statistical middle ground: safer than motorcycle riding per hour of exposure, but carrying risk that demands consistent respect. The great majority of paragliding accidents are preventable. They cluster around a small number of causal patterns, almost all of which involve human decision-making rather than mechanical failure.

The Real Risk Landscape

The British Hang Gliding and Paragliding Association (BHPA) publishes detailed annual accident reports. The consistent findings over the past decade paint a clear picture of where danger actually lies:

  • Flying in unsuitable conditions — this is the leading cause of serious accidents, contributing to approximately 70% of fatalities. "Unsuitable" means conditions beyond the pilot's experience, or beyond the wing's certification, or both.
  • Launching errors — aborted launches, twisted launches, and launches into cross-winds are common causes of low-altitude injuries. Most occur in the first 30 seconds of flight.
  • Stalls and asymmetric collapses — caused by aggressive braking or flying in turbulent air close to the ground. A stall at 20 metres is potentially fatal; a stall at 150 metres is often recoverable.
  • Reserve deployment failures — not because reserves don't work, but because pilots throw them too late, or not at all. Emergency procedures must be drilled until they are automatic.
  • Collision — mid-air collisions at busy sites, and collisions with the terrain during landing approaches.
Honest Assessment

The single most dangerous person in paragliding is not the raw beginner — they are supervised and cautious. The most dangerous pilot is the intermediate, around 50–200 hours, who has gained confidence faster than judgment. This period demands deliberate humility.

Building a Safety Culture

The most effective safety tool is not a better wing or a better helmet — it is a rigorous personal culture of decision-making. Experienced pilots use "go/no-go" checklists before every session. They establish hard limits — wind speed maximums, time-of-day windows, site requirements — and they do not negotiate with those limits when conditions push against them.

The pilot community uses the phrase "fly within your personal envelope." This means: know your actual current skill level (not your aspirational one), and only fly in conditions that envelope comfortably contains. Your envelope expands with experience. But it expands through deliberate training, not through gambling on days that are marginally above your limit.

SIV Training: The Most Important Course After P2

SIV stands for "Simulation d'Incident en Vol" — simulated in-flight incident. It is a training course where a fully qualified pilot deliberately induces stalls, collapses, spins, and spirals in controlled airspace over water, with a safety boat below and an instructor on radio. The goal is not thrill-seeking. The goal is to give your body the muscle memory of recovery before an emergency makes it necessary. After an SIV course, pilots almost universally report feeling dramatically more calm and capable in turbulent air. Book your first SIV course within your first year of flying.

The World's Best Paragliding Destinations in 2026

50+
World-class sites on 6 continents
300+
Flying days/year in Oludeniz
4,800m
Altitude of Bir Billing's primary launch

The world is full of extraordinary flying sites, but certain locations have earned legendary status through a combination of reliable weather, dramatic terrain, excellent infrastructure, and thriving pilot communities. Whether you are taking your first holiday flights as a freshly certified P1, or planning an extended paragliding expedition, these are the destinations that define the sport in 2026.

Oludeniz
Turkey · Mediterranean

Launches from Babadag Mountain at 1,969m, landing on a famous blue lagoon beach. Tandem-heavy but excellent for solos. Perfect 300+ flying days per year. Beginner-friendly with multiple instructors year-round.

Interlaken / Lauterbrunnen
Switzerland · Alps

The postcard Alps. Launch above spectacular valley walls, fly past waterfalls. World-class organisation and landing zones. Expensive, but unforgettable. Best from May to October.

Bir Billing
India · Himachal Pradesh

The "paragliding capital of Asia." Epic Himalayan scenery, strong thermals, affordable. Hosts the Paragliding World Cup. Best season October–November and March–June.

Chamonix
France · Mont Blanc

Advanced terrain requiring experience. Paragliding with direct views of the Mont Blanc massif. Reserved for P2+ pilots. Summer season only. Cable cars access launches easily.

Medellín / Rio Claro
Colombia · South America

Year-round thermals and a growing paragliding scene. Extremely affordable. The Roldanillo area hosts some of South America's best XC flying. Entry visa-free for most nationals.

Queenstown
New Zealand · South Island

Iconic Southern Alps backdrop. Remarkably consistent southerly winds make Coronet Peak a world-class soaring site. A perfect combination with other adventure activities in the region.

Annecy
France · Haute-Savoie

The lake, the mountains, the cheese. Annecy is widely considered the spiritual home of European paragliding. Excellent infrastructure, schools, and the Coupe Icare festival every September.

Ager / Lleida
Spain · Pre-Pyrenees

Consistent strong thermals and long XC routes across the Pyrenean foothills. Host of multiple World Cup legs. The Montsec range provides ridge lift from spring through autumn.

Travel Tip

When travelling to a new site as a beginner, always book a local guide or introductory briefing. Site guides know the local quirks — afternoon valley winds, rotor zones, restricted airspace — that maps and apps don't fully convey. Most sites have a local club or school that offers site briefings for visiting pilots.

Beginner-Best Destinations Shortlist

If you are in your first or second year of flying, your priorities are different from an experienced cross-country pilot. You want: consistent, benign conditions (not too strong, not too turbulent), good infrastructure (easy access, clear landing zones, nearby assistance), a large pilot community (so you can learn from others), and ideally a combination with a good holiday destination for non-flying days. For these reasons, Oludeniz, Annecy, Interlaken, and Bir Billing consistently top beginner destination polls. All four offer access to certified instructors, tandem flights to preview sites before soloing, and a welcoming culture for new pilots.

The first year of paragliding is the most critical and the most exciting. Your skill grows fastest now, your confidence builds rapidly, and every flight is a new milestone. But this is also the period of greatest statistical risk, because confidence and judgment are not the same thing, and they tend to develop at different speeds. The structured calendar below gives you a month-by-month framework for progressing safely through your first season.

This calendar assumes a spring start (April/May in the northern hemisphere) following a beginner course completed in late winter or on a trip. Adapt to your local conditions and always consult your instructor before progressing.

Month 2

Month 3
June		 Extended flights & soaring First flights over 20 minutes. First ridge soaring session. 35+ total flights. Seek ridge soaring opportunities. Practice figure-eights in lift. Attend a club ground-handling session.
Month 4
July		 Thermal awareness & P2 prep Begin to identify and gently work thermals. 50+ total flights. Complete P2 (Club Pilot) assessment if your school offers it. Fly new sites with experienced mentor.
Month 5

Mindset Tip

Keep a detailed flight log — not just the date and location, but the weather conditions, what you worked on, what went well, and what surprised you. This forces deliberate reflection and accelerates learning dramatically. Many experienced pilots credit their flight journal as their most important training tool.

Building Your Pilot Community

Paragliding is not a solo sport in the social sense. The fastest and safest pilots in the world spend enormous time flying with more experienced pilots, watching how they read conditions, how they position themselves in thermals, and how they make go/no-go decisions. Find your local club, volunteer at events, offer to drive retrieve for more experienced pilots on XC days. Every retrieve drive is a free lesson in navigation, weather reading, and long-distance decision-making.

Online communities — the forums at Paraglidingforum.com, national association groups, and the growing presence of pilots on social video platforms — offer a tremendous supplementary resource. But online information has limits. Local knowledge, site-specific guidance, and real-time mentorship from pilots who know your site, your conditions, and your current skill level are irreplaceable. Invest in your local club. It will pay dividends for your entire flying career.

By the end of your first full season, if you have followed a structured progression, flown consistently but not recklessly, completed your SIV training, and integrated yourself into a local pilot community, you will be well on your way to becoming a capable, safe, and joyful paragliding pilot. The sky is patient. It will be there next weekend. Fly within yourself, grow deliberately, and the rewards are boundless.
