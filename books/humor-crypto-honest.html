My cousin, who had previously been unable to balance a checkbook, was now doling out investment advice like a modern-day Warren Buffett. It was as if the rising tide of the bull market had lifted all boats, and everyone on board had been instantly transformed into a financial wizard.

But then, just as suddenly, the market began to tank. Bitcoin plummeted, Ethereum crashed, and the once-mighty crypto titans came tumbling down. And that's when the philosophers emerged. My cousin, the erstwhile genius, was now posting about the impermanence of wealth and the fleeting nature of material success. My coworker, the former financial guru, was waxing poetic about the importance of living in the moment and finding inner peace. It was as if the bear market had triggered some kind of bizarre, reverse-Darwinian evolution, where the smartest and most capable among us were suddenly transformed into pseudo-intellectual, crypto-hippie philosophers.

I must admit, I found this transformation both fascinating and hilarious. Who needs actual knowledge or experience when you can just pivot to philosophy and pretend that you meant to lose all your money all along?

It's like the crypto equivalent of a participation trophy: "I may not have made any money, but I learned a valuable lesson about the importance of mindfulness and the dangers of greed!" As I watched my friends and acquaintances stumble from one absurd, crypto-fueled extreme to the next, I couldn't help but think of the old joke about the economist who couldn't predict the stock market but could always explain why his predictions were wrong.

But what really took the cake was when I saw a former crypto bro, now rebranded as a "philosopher-king," posting about the importance of "hodling" (that's "holding on for dear life" for the uninitiated) not just as an investment strategy, but as a way of life. According to this newfound sage, the true secret to success wasn't about making smart investments or doing your research, but about cultivating a deep sense of inner calm and detachment in the face of market volatility. It was like watching a kind of bizarre, real-time experiment in cognitive dissonance, as my friend struggled to reconcile his own obvious foolishness with his sudden, pretended interest in Eastern spirituality.

As I watched the crypto market continue its wild, unpredictable ride, I couldn't help but think that this was all just a giant, absurd game – a kind of global, financial farce, where the players were all just making it up as they went along. And yet, despite the ridiculousness of it all, I found myself drawn in, helpless to resist the siren song of get-rich-quick schemes and crypto-fueled fantasies. After all, as the great philosopher, Dolly Parton, once said, "If you want the rainbow, you gotta put up with the rain." The acronym DYOR, or "Do Your Own Research," is the ultimate get-out-of-jail-free card for every self-proclaimed crypto guru, Reddit oracle, and enthusiastic but misinformed uncle at the dinner table. It's the four letters that excuse every bad tip, every misguided investment strategy, and every catastrophic loss of funds. I've lost count of how many times I've been told to "DYOR" after asking a simple question about a cryptocurrency's fundamentals, only to be met with a barrage of condescending memes and links to 10-year-old YouTube videos featuring a guy in a bathrobe explaining the "inevitability" of Bitcoin mooning.

But what does DYOR really mean, aside from "I have no idea what I'm talking about, but I'll pretend to be an expert anyway"? Is it a genuine call to action, encouraging us to dig deeper and uncover the hidden truths of the crypto universe? Or is it just a cop-out, a way for armchair experts to deflect responsibility for their own terrible advice? I've tried to take the DYOR approach, I really have. I've spent hours poring over whitepapers, attending webinars, and reading Reddit threads until my eyes glaze over with a mixture of confusion and desperation. And you know what I've learned? That DYOR is just a euphemism for "I'm too lazy to explain this to you, so go figure it out yourself."

I recall one particularly memorable encounter with a DYOR enthusiast who convinced me to invest in a cryptocurrency called "GarlicCoin" (yes, that was its real name). The pitch was simple: GarlicCoin was going to disrupt the entire food industry by creating a blockchain-based platform for garlic farmers to sell their wares. It sounded ridiculous, but the DYOR guy was adamant that it was the future of agriculture. I asked him to explain the tokenomics, and he told me to "DYOR" and pointed me to a 37-page PDF filled with gibberish about "garlic-based smart contracts" and "decentralized onion networks." I invested $100, and of course, GarlicCoin tanked. When I asked the DYOR guy what happened, he just shrugged and said, "You should have done your own research, bro."

"DYOR: the four letters that excuse every bad tip, every misguided investment strategy, and every catastrophic loss of funds. Translation: 'I have no idea what I'm talking about, but follow me anyway!'"

The absurdity of DYOR is that it's often used by people who have clearly done no research themselves. They're like the crypto equivalent of a Google autocomplete result, regurgitating whatever nonsense they've read on Twitter or heard at a conference. And yet, despite the obvious flaws in the DYOR approach, it persists. Maybe it's because, deep down, we all want to believe that we're just one click away from uncovering the next big thing. Maybe it's because we're all secretly hoping to stumble upon a get-rich-quick scheme that doesn't require us to actually do any work. Whatever the reason, DYOR has become the battle cry of the crypto universe, a rallying cry for the uninformed and the misinformed to unite and shout "I have no idea what I'm doing, but follow me anyway!"

As I look back on my own crypto journey, I realize that DYOR has been a constant companion, a reminder that in the wild west of cryptocurrency, nobody really knows what they're doing. We're all just making it up as we go along, hoping to stumble upon a winning formula before the whole thing comes crashing down. So the next time someone tells you to "DYOR," just smile and nod. Because in the world of crypto, the only thing more abundant than bad advice is the willingness to give it. And if you're lucky, you might just stumble upon a GarlicCoin of your own – but don't say I didn't warn you.

Gas Fees and the Philosophy of Sunk Cost

You’ve just spent $47 to retrieve $12. The transaction confirmed three minutes ago, block 18,442,991, base fee 42 gwei. The green checkmark glows on Etherscan like a participation trophy for financial self-harm. You are not okay. But you are also not leaving.

This is the Gas Fee Paradox, and it has nothing to do with petroleum. It occurs when the cost of movement exceeds the value of the cargo—when you pay more to unstake your governance tokens than the tokens are worth, when you bridge $200 to Polygon for a yield farm that dried up in 2022, when you refresh the page seventeen times watching the priority fee spike because you refuse to accept that the network is congested and your dignity is not. You tell yourself you’re “harvesting rewards,” but you’re really just transferring wealth from your checking account to a validator in Estonia who doesn’t know you exist.

The blockchain is philosophically indifferent to your position size. It charges the same toll whether you’re moving ten dollars or ten million. This is supposedly the democratization of finance, which is a beautiful way of saying that the poor pay higher percentages to exit their mistakes. You watch the simulation: $8.40 to claim $9.20 in farming rewards. You click confirm anyway. Why? Because you’ve already paid $12.50 to stake them. To stop now would be to admit that the first $12.50 was a mistake, and you’d rather light another $8.40 on fire than face that truth. This is called the Sunk Cost Fallacy, and in crypto, we’ve rebranded it as “Diamond Hands.”

There is a specific type of person who bridges to Arbitrum to save $3 on a swap, paying $22 in bridge fees to do so. There is a specific type of person who will ride a position to zero because “I’ve already paid the gas to get in.” You are that person. You have paid $340 in cumulative gas fees over six months to maintain a $200 position in a dog-themed governance token. You are not an investor. You are a digital Sisyphus, except instead of a boulder, you’re pushing a shopping cart with one wobbly wheel up a hill made of gwei, and every time you near the summit, someone launches a memecoin and the base fee spikes.

But here is the terrible secret: this reveals something admirable about you. You are stubborn in a world of liquidity. You have committed to the bit. While others panic-sold at a loss, you stayed, not because you believed in the fundamentals, but because you’d already paid $85 in gas and you’d be damned if you’d pay another $85 to leave. You have become a philosopher of spite, a practitioner of sunk-cost theology. You don’t want the money back; you want the network to know you’re still here.

"You’ve just spent $47 to retrieve $12. The green checkmark glows on Etherscan like a participation trophy for financial self-harm. You are not okay. But you are also not leaving."

Welcome to the congregation. We meet every time gas drops below 20 gwei, which is never. Pass the collection plate. It’s empty, but the transaction still cost $14.

Taxes, Airdrops, and the Art of Pretending It Didn't Happen

The human brain is a marvelous defense mechanism. It can suppress childhood traumas, erase the lyrics to entire albums, and—most crucially for our purposes—completely obliterate the memory of purchasing Ethereum at $4,800 because a guy on Twitter with a cartoon ape avatar said it was "guaranteed alpha." This neurological phenomenon, known to crypto accountants as *Selective Memory Syndrome*, allows you to approach April 15th with the serene confidence of a man who has definitely, absolutely, only ever bought the dip.

Let us discuss airdrops, which are essentially the IRS’s way of testing if you’re paying attention. You clicked a button in 2021 and received 400 tokens of something called $ASSBLOC. At the moment of receipt—because the tax code is written by people who have never used Uniswap—these were worth $8,000. You did not sell them, because you are a *builder*, not a *seller*. By December, $ASSBLOC was worth $4.50 and the founder had changed his Twitter bio to “Digital Nomad | Seeking Opportunities.” Congratulations: you owe capital gains on $8,000 of income you never actually possessed, based on a blockchain event that occurred while you were in the bathroom.

"In crypto, there are only two certainties: death and taxes — and at least death only happens once."

This brings us to cost basis, or as I like to call it, *The Impossible Math*. You bought Bitcoin in 2017 on Coinbase, moved it to Binance in 2019, swapped half for Cardano during a manic episode in 2021, staked the rest on a protocol that was hacked by a teenager in Belarus, and then “bridged” the remnants to Arbitrum where they currently sit, inaccessible, because you wrote the seed phrase on a napkin that went through the wash. Your accountant—who specializes in “traditional finance” and has started drinking at her desk—has asked for your “transaction history.” You have seventeen CSV files with conflicting timestamps and a screenshot of a gas fee that cost $400. You are considering faking your own death.

Then there is DeFi yield farming, which is essentially farming taxable events at industrial scale. You provided liquidity to a pool. You received rewards. You compounded them. You suffered impermanent loss. You exited the pool. Each of these actions is a distinct taxable event, meaning you have generated approximately 347 separate capital gains calculations for a net loss of $23 and a profound sense of existential dread. The IRS does not care that you were “learning.” The IRS does not accept “NGMI” as a deduction. The IRS wants to know why you traded a monkey JPEG for 3.2 ETH and then immediately used that ETH to buy a different monkey JPEG that was, objectively, uglier.

Here is the practical guide: First, locate your highest conviction trade—the one that actually made money. Print that statement in 24-point font and tape it to your monitor. This is your *Emotional Anchor*. Next, gather every other document and place them in a shoebox labeled “2021-2023: The Lost Years.” When your accountant asks about the box, develop a sudden interest in boating accidents. Finally, remember that in crypto, there are only two certainties: death and taxes, and at least death only happens once.

Remember: the blockchain never forgets, but you certainly can try.
