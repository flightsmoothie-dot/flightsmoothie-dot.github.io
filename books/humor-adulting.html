Taxes: A 30-Year Sentence for Being Born

As I sat at my kitchen table, surrounded by receipts, W-2s, and a half-eaten pizza that was now congealing into a science experiment, I couldn't help but feel like I was serving a 30-year sentence for the crime of being born. Taxes, it seemed, were the ultimate punishment for the audacity of existing. I mean, what's the logic behind forcing people to spend their precious time on earth calculating deductions and credits, only to hand over a significant chunk of their hard-earned cash to the government? It's like the universe is saying, "Hey, you know what would be a great way to spend your Saturday afternoon? Not watching Netflix, not playing video games, but instead trying to decipher the cryptic language of tax code." And don't even get me started on the irony of having to pay for the privilege of being taxed – it's like being charged a cover fee to enter a bad nightclub.

"I couldn't help but feel like I was serving a 30-year sentence for the crime of being born. Taxes: the ultimate punishment for the audacity of existing."

I stared blankly at my tax return, trying to make sense of the endless rows of numbers and codes, feeling like a cryptologist trying to crack the Enigma code. Why did I need to itemize my deductions? What even constitutes a "qualified expense"? And what's the difference between a 1099 and a W-2, aside from the fact that one of them makes me feel like a freelance hustler and the other makes me feel like a corporate drone? As I delved deeper into the tax rabbit hole, I began to wonder if the entire system was just a giant social experiment designed to drive people to the brink of insanity. "Let's see how many hours we can get people to spend on tax preparation before they start questioning the meaning of life," the government seems to be saying. And honestly, I'm surprised I haven't yet received a letter from the IRS informing me that I owe them my firstborn child.

As I struggled to reconcile my bank statements and calculate my charitable donations, I couldn't help but think about all the other things I could be doing with my time. I could be learning a new language, or practicing yoga, or even just staring at a wall and contemplating the meaninglessness of existence. But no, instead I was stuck inside on a beautiful spring day, trying to make sense of the Byzantine tax code. It's like the government is trying to suck the joy out of life, one deduction at a time. And don't even get me started on the "tax professionals" who claim to be able to navigate this mess with ease. I'm pretty sure they're just a bunch of wizards who have mastered the dark art of tax magic, and are now laughing all the way to the bank as they charge me an arm and a leg to do my taxes.

At one point, I found myself pondering the existential implications of tax season. Is this really what it means to be an adult? Is this what our ancestors fought and died for – the right to spend hours each year filling out forms and crunching numbers? It's a bleak vision of the future, one in which humanity is reduced to anever-ending cycle of tax preparation and compliance. And yet, as I sat at my kitchen table, surrounded by the detritus of tax season, I couldn't help but feel a twisted sense of solidarity with my fellow taxpayers. We're all in this together, it seems – stuck in a never-ending cycle of paperwork and frustration, united in our shared misery.

As I finally finished my taxes and clicked the "submit" button, I felt a mix of relief and despair. Relief that the ordeal was over, but despair at the thought of having to do it all again next year. And the year after that. And the year after that. It's a Sisyphean task, one that will continue to haunt me for the rest of my life. But hey, at least I'll have my refund to look forward to – a small consolation prize for surviving another year of tax season. As I stood up from my kitchen table, stretched my arms, and walked away from the wreckage of tax season, I couldn't help but wonder what other joys adulthood had in store for me. Bring on the mortgage payments and the health insurance premiums – I'm ready to adult like a pro.

Cooking for One (A Tragedy in Three Acts)

The art of cooking for one: a culinary purgatory where the only thing more abundant than the leftovers is the sense of desperation. It's like trying to solve a math problem that never quite adds up – 1 person, 4 servings, and a nagging feeling that you're single-handedly keeping the Tupperware industry afloat. I've lost count of the number of times I've stood in front of the fridge, staring down at a container of congealed pasta, wondering if I could really bring myself to eat it for the fifth time that week. The answer, of course, is always yes – because when you're cooking for one, the only thing more pathetic than eating last night's dinner is ordering takeout... again.

"Cooking for one: a culinary purgatory where the only thing more abundant than the leftovers is the sense of desperation."

As I navigated the aisles of my local grocery store, I found myself confronted with the usual array of "singles-friendly" packaging – those laughable, miniature versions of regular products that seem to exist solely to rub it in your face that you're, well, alone. The "Fun Size" snack packs, the "Individual Serving" frozen meals, the " Solo Cup" coffee containers... it's like the marketing team got together and said, "You know what would be hilarious? If we made all our products look like they were designed for a sad, solitary child's lunchbox!" And yet, I still find myself drawn to these pathetic little portions, if only because they seem to whisper sweet nothings in my ear – "You're not a failure, you're just... efficient with your resources."

But the real tragedy of cooking for one isn't the food itself – it's the crushing sense of monotony that comes with eating the same meal, alone, every night. I've started to feel like I'm trapped in some kind of bizarre, gastronomic Groundhog Day, where the only thing that changes is the day of the week and the type of takeout container on my coffee table. Monday: pizza box. Tuesday: Chinese food carton. Wednesday: the faint scent of desperation wafting from the depths of my kitchen. It's like I'm stuck in a never-ending cycle of culinary boredom, and the only way to break free is to... well, invite someone over for dinner, I suppose. But that would require actual human interaction, and let's be real, I'm not exactly overflowing with social invitations at the moment.

As I stood in my kitchen, staring down at a sizzling pan of what could only be described as "questionable life choices" (i.e., a solo serving of scrambled eggs, a can of sardines, and a dash of hope), I had an epiphany: cooking for one isn't just about food – it's about the existential crisis that comes with realizing you're the only person who truly cares about your meal plan. It's about confronting the abyss of solitude, one sad, pathetic meal at a time. And in that moment, something strange happened: I felt a sense of liberation, a sense of freedom to eat whatever, whenever, without fear of judgment or reprisal. I mean, who needs human connection when you've got a perfectly good can of sardines and a willingness to ignore the creeping sense of despair?

In the end, it all comes down to this: cooking for one is a tragedy, but it's also a kind of twisted, masochistic freedom. It's the freedom to eat an entire pizza by yourself, to watch an entire season of TV in one sitting, to wear the same sweatpants for three days straight. And if that's not the definition of adulthood, I don't know what is. So the next time you find yourself standing in front of the fridge, staring down at a container of congealed leftovers, just remember: you're not alone – you're just... efficiently resourceful. And if all else fails, you can always order takeout... again.

Your Credit Score Has Opinions About Your Life Choices

I recently checked my credit score, and let's just say it was a real wake-up call. It was like my credit score was saying, "Girl, you've been living off of ramen noodles and daydreams for far too long. It's time to get your financial life together." I mean, I know I've made some questionable decisions in the past – like that time I bought a vintage typewriter on eBay for $200, or the great "I'm going to learn how to play the harmonica" debacle of 2018. But hey, a girl's gotta have some hobbies, right?

But seriously, have you ever tried to read the fine print on a credit card agreement? It's like trying to decipher a secret code written by a mischievous group of lawyers. "If you miss a payment, we'll charge you an interest rate of 25% and also make you watch a never-ending loop of commercials for timeshares in Boca Raton." I'm pretty sure that's what it says, anyway. And don't even get me started on the part where they explain how they calculate your credit score.

It's like they're speaking a different language – "We'll take into account your payment history, credit utilization ratio, and also the alignment of the stars and the number of likes you get on Instagram." I swear, it's like they're trying to confuse us on purpose.

"Your credit score has opinions about your life choices, and honestly, it's a bit of a judgmental aunt. 'Oh, you think you can afford that avocado toast? Think again, kiddo.'"

I've been trying to improve my credit score, but it's like trying to get a cat to do tricks for treats. It's all, "Oh, you want me to pay my bills on time? Well, maybe I will, maybe I won't. But first, can you explain to me why I need to care about my credit score in the first place?" And then I'm all, "Uh, well, because you need good credit to buy a house or a car or... or... um, a really expensive pair of shoes?" And the cat is all, "Pffft, shoes? Who needs shoes when you have catnip and a sunny windowsill?" I mean, I guess that's a fair point. But still, I'd like to be able to buy a house one day without having to sell my soul to the bank.

I've been reading all these articles about how to boost your credit score, and they all say the same thing: pay your bills on time, don't open too many credit cards, and for the love of all things good and holy, do not buy a timeshare in Boca Raton. But what they don't tell you is that it's hard to resist the temptation of buying things you don't need when you're feeling stressed or bored. Like, have you ever scrolled through Instagram and seen one of those ads for a "life-changing" essential oil diffuser or a " revolutionary" new type of yoga mat? And you're all, "Ooh, that sounds amazing! I need that in my life!" And then you click the link and realize it's just a $50 piece of plastic with some fancy words on the packaging. Yeah, that's not helping my credit score.

I guess what I'm trying to say is that my credit score and I are in a long-term relationship, and it's complicated. Sometimes it's all, "I love you, baby, you're doing great!" And other times it's all, "Uh, what were you thinking, buying that vintage typewriter? You're never going to use it!" But despite all the ups and downs, I'm trying to make it work. I'm paying my bills on time, I'm not buying too many unnecessary things, and I'm even considering getting a timeshare in Boca Raton (just kidding, that's never going to happen). And who knows, maybe one day my credit score and I will be happy and healthy, and I'll be able to buy a house with a big yard and a sunny windowsill for my cat to lounge on. A girl can dream, right?

'I'll Deal With It Tomorrow' — The Complete Works

I've come to realize that my personal mantra, "I'll deal with it tomorrow," is not so much a procrastination technique as it is a clever form of time travel. By putting off tasks until the next day, I'm essentially creating a temporary wormhole that allows me to escape the drudgery of adulting for a full 24 hours. It's like I'm telling my responsibilities, "You know what, I'm not going to deal with you today. Instead, I'll send my future self to handle it, and I'm sure they'll be thrilled to pick up the slack." Of course, my future self is always shocked and appalled by the sheer volume of tasks that past me has so thoughtfully bequeathed to them.

"'I'll deal with it tomorrow' is not procrastination — it's time travel. By putting off tasks, you create a temporary wormhole that lets you escape adulting for a full 24 hours."

As I've honed this skill over the years, I've begun to notice that "I'll deal with it tomorrow" has become an all-purpose solution to, well, everything. Need to do laundry? I'll deal with it tomorrow. Have a looming work deadline? Tomorrow's got this. Want to avoid an awkward conversation with a friend or family member? You guessed it – tomorrow's the day. It's like I've created a magical realm where time stands still, and the only thing that exists is the perpetual promise of a better, more responsible tomorrow. And yet, somehow, tomorrow always seems to turn into today, and I'm left scrambling to deal with the detritus of my own procrastination.

One of the most fascinating aspects of "I'll deal with it tomorrow" is the way it warps my perception of time. I've started to think of my life as a series of overlapping tomorrow-bubbles, each one representing a task or responsibility that I've cleverly deferred to the next day. It's like I'm living in a never-ending game of temporal Jenga, where each new tomorrow-bubble is precariously balanced on top of the last, threatening to come crashing down at any moment. And yet, somehow, I manage to keep the whole rickety structure upright, largely through a combination of caffeine, denial, and a stubborn refusal to acknowledge the inevitable collapse of my entire tomorrow-based ecosystem.

Despite its many benefits, "I'll deal with it tomorrow" is not without its drawbacks. For one thing, it's led to a series of surreal and often embarrassing encounters with utility company representatives, who seem to take great pleasure in informing me that my electricity/gas/water bill is overdue – again. It's like they're speaking a different language, one that doesn't involve the sacred concept of tomorrow. "But I'll deal with it tomorrow," I protest, only to be met with a stern lecture about the importance of timely payment and the perils of procrastination. I've started to wonder if these people have never heard of the tomorrow-bubble theory, or if they're simply immune to its charms.

As I sit here, surrounded by the detritus of my own procrastination, I'm forced to confront the disturbing possibility that "I'll deal with it tomorrow" may not be the foolproof system I thought it was. In fact, it may be nothing more than a cleverly disguised form of self-delusion, a way of avoiding the uncomfortable truth that adulthood is hard, and sometimes you just have to deal with things today. But even as I contemplate this harsh reality, I find myself thinking, "You know what? I'll deal with it tomorrow." After all, tomorrow is always just a day away – and who knows, maybe by then I'll have finally mastered the art of time travel.

Sleep: The Luxury You Can't Afford to Buy

Sleeping In died quietly on a Tuesday, approximately three years after you started paying your own utilities. It was 26, maybe 26-and-a-half. The cause of death was listed as "natural causes," which is medical terminology for "you agreed to a 9:00 AM stand-up meeting on a Friday and voluntarily set an alarm for 6:45 on Saturday to 'get a jump on errands.'" There was no obituary. You were too tired to write one.

Remember when sleeping in meant something? It was a physical achievement, a competitive sport. You'd emerge from a blackout-cave at 2:47 PM on a Sunday, blinded by daylight, having achieved REM cycles that felt like time travel. Your only responsibility was deciding between dining hall pancakes or regret. Now, your body betrays you with the punctuality of a German train conductor. You will wake up at 7:15 AM on a Saturday with the specific heart rate of someone who just remembered they replied "Sounds good!" to an email they didn't fully read. You will stare at the ceiling, calculating exactly how many hours remain until Monday, as if math has ever helped anyone relax.

The cruelest joke is that you can finally afford the sleep. You bought the $800 mattress. The linen sheets with the thread count that requires scientific notation. The gravity blanket that simulates the hug your parents stopped giving you when you moved out. You have created a sleep environment that rivals a sensory deprivation tank, and you use it for exactly four hours and fifty-three minutes while your brain plays a highlight reel of every awkward thing you've said since 2019. You are rich in Egyptian cotton and destitute in unconsciousness.

"You bought the $800 mattress. The linen sheets. The gravity blanket that simulates the hug your parents stopped giving you when you moved out. You are rich in Egyptian cotton and destitute in unconsciousness."

We must also acknowledge the Sunday Scaries, which have evolved from a vague melancholy into a 4:00 AM biological alarm that cannot be snoozed. You will wake up convinced you forgot to submit the quarterly report, or that you left the stove on, or that you simply exist wrong. You will check your phone, confirm that everything is technically fine, and then spend forty-five minutes watching a TikTok of a man restoring a 1970s Honda while your cortisol levels spike high enough to power a small village.

The sleep industrial complex knows you're desperate. They sell you blue-light glasses that make you look like a minor character in a sci-fi movie, melatonin gummies shaped like tiny moons that taste like despair, and white noise machines that simulate "rain on a tin roof" because apparently your $2,400/month apartment with paper-thin walls isn't atmospheric enough. You have purchased meditation apps you use twice, then abandon like gym memberships, their soothing British narrators whispering into the void while you panic-scroll LinkedIn at midnight.

You have become the person who says "I slept in until eight" with the pride of someone who just ran a marathon. You tell your coworkers you "caught up on sleep" over the weekend, which is a lie. You caught up on laundry, on meal prep, on the existential dread of realizing you need to schedule a dentist appointment six months in advance. Sleep is now a luxury good, like vintage wine or affordable housing—technically available, theoretically yours, but always just out of stock.

Rest in peace, Sleeping In. You were too beautiful for this world, and we were too caffeinated to save you.

Health Insurance: A Choose Your Own Adventure

You are sitting at your desk when a manila envelope lands with the thud of a foreclosure notice. Inside: a booklet glossy enough to suggest importance but thin enough to guarantee disappointment. Your employer has provided three healthcare options, which is two more than your brain can process before coffee.

**If you choose the High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP), turn to page 23.** Here you learn that you will pay $180 per month to achieve the privilege of paying $7,500 out of pocket before insurance acknowledges your existence. It's marketed as "consumer-driven healthcare," which is corporate speak for "we hope you forget you have appendicitis." The booklet suggests you pair this with a Health Savings Account, as if you have savings, or health.

**If you choose the PPO with the low deductible, turn to page 47.** Congratulations. You have selected the "Gold Plan," which costs $340 monthly and covers 80% of costs after you pay $1,500, unless you go out-of-network, in which case it covers 0% and also mails your parents a letter explaining your poor life choices. You spend twenty minutes calculating whether you're more likely to break a leg skiing or develop a stress ulcer from calculating premiums. Math was not supposed to follow you out of high school like this.

**If you attempt to understand what a deductible actually is, close the book.** Smash it against your desk until the staples loosen. Studies show that 94% of adults who successfully comprehend their deductible immediately develop the ability to see through time, mostly to witness their own bankruptcy.

"The booklet suggests you pair this with a Health Savings Account, as if you have savings, or health."

You turn to page 47 because the HR representative said it was "popular with people your age," which is code for "we saw you eating ramen at your desk." The page explains that once you meet your deductible, coinsurance kicks in at 20%, but only for in-network providers, and only after you've satisfied your separate prescription deductible, which is different from your medical deductible, which resets in January or possibly March if Mercury is in retrograde during a leap year.

You flip to page 92 to clarify "out-of-pocket maximum." The book informs you that your out-of-pocket max is $8,000, which does not include your premiums, your dental, your vision, or your will to live. It does, however, include the $400 you spent on that urgent care visit when you thought you had mono but it was just capitalism. You owe them $380 because the doctor was in-network but the room was "out-of-network," a distinction that suggests healthcare operates on Dungeons & Dragons alignment charts.

Turn to page 12 to select a primary care physician from a directory where 40% of the doctors are retired, 30% aren't accepting new patients, and the remaining 30% have appointment availability in 2024 BC. You pick a name that sounds comforting—Dr. Elaine Soft blankets—and immediately forget every detail of your plan until you break your ankle at a destination wedding in Cabo, at which point you will discover that "worldwide coverage" specifically excludes sand.

In the end, you select the middle option, pay $287 monthly, and develop a superstitious ritual of touching wood whenever you pass a hospital. You file the booklet in a drawer between your tax returns and your abandoned dream of owning a house. Six months later, you will receive a bill for $1,200 for a blood test because the lab was "in-network" but the specific vial was "out-of-network," and you will stare at the receipt until it makes less sense than when you started.

The adventure, it turns out, chose you.
